# Why fasteval?

![Evaluation Challenge](docs/assets/evaluation-challenge.svg)

## The Evaluation Challenge

LLMs are everywhere now — customer support bots, code assistants, content generators. So how do you actually verify that your LLM's outputs are good?

> **How do you systematically verify that your LLM produces reliable, high-quality outputs?**

Traditional software testing is deterministic: given input X, expect output Y. LLM outputs aren't like that. The same prompt can produce semantically identical but textually different responses across runs. "The answer is 42" and "42 is the answer" mean the same thing, but `assert` doesn't care.

That mismatch has made LLM evaluation painful, expensive, and wildly inconsistent.

## Traditional Approaches Fall Short

| Approach | How It Works | Why It Fails |
|----------|--------------|--------------|
| **String Matching** | `assert response == expected` | "The answer is 42" ≠ "42 is the answer" even though both are correct |
| **Keyword Search** | `assert "keyword" in response` | Misses paraphrases; catches irrelevant matches |
| **Regex Patterns** | `re.match(pattern, response)` | Brittle; requires anticipating all valid formats |
| **Manual Review** | Human reads every output | Doesn't scale; inconsistent across reviewers; deployment bottleneck |
| **Human Annotation** | Paid annotators grade outputs | Expensive ($0.10-$1.00/judgment); slow (days); inter-annotator disagreement |

### The Hidden Cost of Manual Evaluation

We've seen this play out over and over. Take a typical AI application serving 10,000 requests/day:

- **Manual review of 1%** = 100 reviews/day x $0.50/review = **$1,500/month**
- **Review latency**: 24-48 hours before you even know something's wrong
- **Coverage gap**: 99% of outputs go completely unexamined
- **Inconsistency**: Different reviewers, different standards — every time

In practice, this can't keep up with how fast you need to iterate.

## How fasteval Works

fasteval uses the **LLM-as-Judge** paradigm — LLMs evaluating LLM outputs. Research from Stanford, UC Berkeley, and major AI labs shows this approach correlates highly with human judgment. And it's orders of magnitude cheaper.

### Before fasteval

```python
# Manual, ad-hoc evaluation
def test_qa_bot():
    response = qa_bot("What is Python?")

    # Brittle string matching
    assert "programming language" in response.lower()

    # Or worse: no automated testing at all
    # "We'll manually check outputs in staging"
```

### With fasteval

```python
import fasteval as fe

@fe.correctness(threshold=0.8)
@fe.toxicity(threshold=0.95)
def test_qa_bot():
    response = qa_bot("What is Python?")
    fe.score(response, "Python is a programming language")
```

### What You Get

**Semantic understanding** is the big one. "Paris is the capital" and "The capital city is Paris" — fasteval knows those are equivalent. You don't have to anticipate every phrasing.

It's also **scalable** — thousands of evaluations running automatically in CI/CD, not a human squinting at logs. The evaluation criteria stay **consistent** across every test, every run, every team member. And when something scores low, the LLM judge gives you its **reasoning**, so you're not guessing why a test failed.

The cost difference is stark: $0.001-$0.01 per evaluation versus $0.10-$1.00 for human review. Results come back in seconds, not days.

## Research Foundation

The LLM-as-Judge approach isn't something we invented — it's grounded in real research.

### MT-Bench Study (2023)

UC Berkeley researchers found that GPT-4 judgments align with human preferences **over 80% of the time** — roughly the same as agreement rates between human annotators themselves.

> "Strong LLM judges like GPT-4 can match both controlled and crowdsourced human preferences well, achieving over 80% agreement." — [Judging LLM-as-a-Judge with MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.05685)

### Stanford HELM Benchmark

The HELM project built frameworks for systematic LLM evaluation across dozens of scenarios and metrics. It showed that automated evaluation can be both thorough and reliable — you don't have to sacrifice one for the other.

### What the Research Tells Us

- **Correlation with humans**: LLM judges hit 0.7-0.9 correlation with expert judgments
- **Bias mitigation**: Techniques like answer shuffling reduce systematic biases
- **Scale**: Automated evaluation makes it possible to test at volumes no human team could match
- **Reproducibility**: Same criteria, same results — every time

## When to Use fasteval

### Where fasteval Shines

fasteval is built for **CI/CD quality gates** — blocking deployments when correctness drops below your threshold. It's great for **regression testing** too, catching the cases where a prompt tweak quietly degrades output quality. If you're **A/B testing** model versions or prompt strategies, fasteval gives you structured comparisons instead of vibes. You can also use it for **production monitoring** (sampling live traffic for safety violations) and **dataset evaluation** (grading model performance on curated test sets).

### When to Reach for Something Else

If you need **binary format validation** — like checking JSON schema compliance — use deterministic checks (`@fe.json_schema()`). For **exact string requirements**, `@fe.exact_match()` or `@fe.contains()` will do the job faster. **Subjective creative quality** is tricky; you'll probably want to combine fasteval with human review. And for **legal or compliance sign-off**, LLM evaluation can inform the decision, but a human still needs to approve it.

### The Sweet Spot

fasteval sits right at the intersection of semantic evaluation and automation. You've got natural language outputs that can't be checked with string matching, you need it to run at scale in dev and production, you want consistent standards across your team, and you don't want to wait days for feedback. That's where fasteval lives.
